3. Severity vs the wearable-IMU UPDRS literature

What's unusual about our H1

Most published wearable-IMU UPDRS papers do not deploy SSL ranking with leaf-index features. H1 was particularly aggressive — a 0.34 CCC inflation is on the high end for a single leakage path. In that sense, our pre-audit pipeline was more leaky than the typical published paper.

What's typical of the literature

Patterns in the H2-class — global preprocessing (imputation / scaling / feature selection on full X), single-vector hyperparameter tuning, calibration tuned on the test set, retrieval pools that include the test subject — are widespread and rarely audited. Each typically inflates CCC by 0.01–0.15. Compounded, a paper without an explicit 5-null gate could plausibly carry +0.05 to +0.20 CCC of unattributed inflation.

So: our largest single leak (H1) was unusually severe, but the broader leakage class is endemic to the field. The closest published comparators (Hssayeni 2021 r=0.79 on N=24 LOOCV total Part III; D'Cruz 2023 r=0.82 / ICC=0.76) report no formal audit, so the headline numbers should be read as upper bounds on the true inductive performance under their protocols.

5. Publication choices

A — Cautionary benchmark (current framing) Sci Reports / npj Parkinson's / Sci Data

Lead with: first published WearGait-PD UPDRS-III strict-inductive regression + negative findings catalog + structural learning-curve ceiling + LOOCV→LOSO→cross-dataset transportability cliff. Current framing uses T1 iter34 only as a caveated candidate (CCC = 0.7366, N=93) and T3 iter47 valid-range as the current headline (CCC = 0.3784, LOSO = 0.150).

Methodologically defensible end-to-end. Novel contribution to the field. Leverages F65/F73 audits as differentiation. Aligns with FDA-CDRH framing of WearGait-PD as a benchmark.

Absolute numbers below published wearable-IMU SOTA. Reviewer 2 may push for external validation (Hssayeni MJFF) which is currently DUA-blocked.

Best fit when: the goal is a citable benchmark contribution and the journal values methodological honesty. Lowest scientific risk; moderate clinical impact.

B — Methods-first audit paper Nature Machine Intelligence / JMLR / TMLR

Lead with: empirical scenario-A vs scenario-C comparison on the same dataset; quantify each leakage class. Position the +0.343 CCC of H1 inflation as the central finding, not the post-audit benchmark.

High-impact venue. Generalizes beyond Parkinson's — leakage taxonomy applies to any wearable-target regression task. Pre-existing audit infrastructure (P1–P5 5-null gate) is the methodological deliverable.

Harder pitch: needs at least one additional dataset to argue generalizability. Clinical readers may see it as a niche-methods paper. Longer review cycle.

Best fit when: we can replicate the audit on at least one external dataset (Hssayeni MJFF, MJFF Levodopa Response, mPower) within 2–3 months.

C — Norm-strict headline + audit appendix Movement Disorders / Lancet Digital Health

Lead with Pearson r (literature norm) on T1 and T3, while explicitly labeling T1 as a caveated candidate and T3 as the lower valid-range corrected result. Audit relegated to a methods supplement.

Numbers comparable to Hssayeni 2021 r=0.79 and D'Cruz 2023 r=0.82 — competitive on the same metric. Easier acceptance at a clinical journal. Faster path.

Erases the cautionary-benchmark contribution. Methodological work becomes a footnote. Clinically oriented reviewers may not value the pre-registration / multi-comparisons rigor and will compare on absolute number alone (where we lose).

Best fit when: the priority is fastest possible publication and we accept losing the audit narrative as a primary contribution.

D — Wait for external validation, then dual-submit npj Parkinson's (clinical) + JMLR (methods)

Apply for Synapse DUA on Hssayeni MJFF or MJFF Levodopa Response (typical 1–3 day approval), run cross-cohort iter34, then submit a clinical paper anchored on within+across cohort numbers AND a methods paper anchored on the audit. Reuse the same audit infrastructure across both.

Strongest possible evidence. External validation defuses the transportability concern that's the largest weakness of options A/B/C. Two papers from one body of work.

2–3 months minimum delay. DUA approval is not guaranteed. Risk of overlap concerns between the two papers. External cohort may not transfer cleanly (per F60b PADS evidence).

Best fit when: there is no near-term submission deadline and the budget supports two parallel writing efforts.

E — Preprint + arXiv only (priority claim) arXiv / medRxiv

Skip peer review for now. Post the cautionary-benchmark version to medRxiv with full audit. Establishes priority on the leakage taxonomy and the iter34 result while we decide between A–D.

Immediate; no reviewer cycle. Establishes priority. Allows community feedback before formal submission. Compatible with later submission to A, B, C, or D.

No peer-review imprimatur. May reduce eventual journal interest if the preprint reaches saturation in citations / community discussion before submission.

Best fit when: the priority claim matters and we want public review feedback before committing to a journal target.

6. Recommendation

The dominant question is whether external validation is achievable in the next 60–90 days. If yes, option D (wait + dual-submit) dominates: it converts the current single-cohort cautionary paper into a stronger clinical paper plus a separable methods paper. If external validation is blocked indefinitely (Synapse DUA denied), option A (cautionary benchmark) is the safest single-paper outcome and preserves the audit contribution. Option E (preprint) is compatible with all of A/B/C/D and reduces priority risk with no downside; recommended in parallel with whichever path is chosen.

Option C is not recommended as a primary path because it sacrifices the contribution that makes the work distinctive. Option B is recommended only if D is infeasible and we can secure at least one external benchmark dataset within the methods-paper review window.
