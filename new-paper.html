Observable Motor Severity from Gait Inertial Sensors:
Subdomain UPDRS-III Regression on WearGait-PD

[Authors] — March 2026

Dataset: WearGait-PD (Anderson et al., Scientific Data, 2026)

Abstract

We present the first regression-based prediction of MDS-UPDRS Part III motor scores from body-worn inertial measurement units (IMUs) on the WearGait-PD dataset (N=178 subjects, 13 sensors, 100 Hz), establishing three tiers of results. First, total UPDRS-III prediction from IMU features alone achieves MAE=8.21±0.41 (10-split stack), bounded by the ~40% of score variance attributable to clinically unobservable items (rigidity, speech, facial expression). Second, a targeted observable gait subdomain model predicting only IMU-visible items (7–14: toe tapping through body bradykinesia) achieves MAE=3.21±0.43 on a 40-point subscale—with r=0.754 on held-out test. Third, a clinician-augmented model incorporating ground-truth observable subscores achieves MAE=3.97±0.43 (p=0.002 vs. baseline) for total UPDRS-III. We demonstrate the robustness of the ~8-point MAE ceiling through a comprehensive ablation study spanning 1,875 features, seven feature groups, three deep learning architectures, item-level decomposition, and mixture-of-experts gating—none of which improved on the baseline under multi-split validation. This ceiling is a structural consequence of unobservable UPDRS items, not a modeling limitation. These findings decompose the observability ceiling in UPDRS-III prediction and establish the first benchmark for this dataset.

Keywords: Parkinson's disease, MDS-UPDRS Part III, inertial measurement units, gait analysis, motor severity regression, WearGait-PD, observable subdomain, feature ablation

1. Introduction

Parkinson's disease (PD) is the second most common neurodegenerative disorder, affecting over 10 million people worldwide. The Movement Disorder Society–Unified Parkinson's Disease Rating Scale Part III (MDS-UPDRS-III) is the clinical gold standard for motor assessment, comprising 18 items (33 sub-scores) rated 0–4 each, yielding a total score of 0–132. However, UPDRS-III evaluation requires trained neurologists, is inherently subjective, and captures only a snapshot at the time of clinical visit.

Body-worn inertial measurement units (IMUs) offer continuous, objective motor monitoring during naturalistic movement. The emergence of large-scale datasets like WearGait-PD1 (178 subjects, 13 body-worn sensors at 100 Hz, full UPDRS-III scores) enables the first rigorous investigation of UPDRS-III regression from gait IMU on this cohort. Prior work has been limited to N=24 cohorts2,3 or has suffered from methodological issues including window-level data leakage4 and within-subject cross-validation contamination5.

A fundamental but underexplored challenge is that total UPDRS-III includes items that are inherently unobservable from gait-mounted IMU sensors. Rigidity (item 3) requires hands-on examination. Speech (item 1) and facial expression (item 2) are not captured by body-worn accelerometers. Finger tapping (item 4), hand movements (item 5), and pronation-supination (item 6) produce minimal signal during gait tasks. This creates a theoretical prediction ceiling that no amount of feature engineering or model complexity can overcome.

In this work, we make three contributions:

  1. First WearGait-PD regression benchmark with rigorous evaluation methodology, establishing MAE=8.21 for total UPDRS-III from IMU alone.
  2. Observable subdomain decomposition showing that the gait-observable items (7–14) can be predicted at MAE=3.21 on a 40-point subscale, while unobservable items are bounded at MAE=5.80.
  3. Comprehensive ablation study across 1,875 features, 7 feature groups, 3 deep learning architectures, item-level decomposition, mixture-of-experts gating, and rigorous 10-split validation, revealing that hand-crafted time-frequency features remain optimal and the ~8-point MAE ceiling is robust to all tested approaches at this sample size (N=178).

2.2 Subdomain Prediction

Subdomain-level prediction of UPDRS-III has received limited attention. Kim et al.8 demonstrated that axial symptoms (items 27–30 in the old UPDRS, corresponding to gait, posture, and postural stability) achieve R²=0.77 from gait video alone, substantially exceeding total UPDRS-III (R²=0.59). Shah et al.9 showed that the Postural Instability and Gait Difficulty (PIGD) subscore can be predicted from 3 lumbar/foot IMU sensors during free-living monitoring. Pang et al.10 predicted UPDRS-III subitems from hand-mounted IMUs during clinical tasks, achieving AUROC 68–92% for motor score classification. No prior work has performed IMU-based regression for observable gait subitems on a dataset of our scale.

2.3 The Observability Ceiling

MDS-UPDRS Part III contains 18 items, of which we classify 8 as observable from gait IMU (items 7–14: toe tapping, leg agility, arising from chair, gait, freezing of gait, postural stability, posture, body bradykinesia) and 10 as unobservable (items 1–6, 15–18: speech, facial expression, rigidity, upper-limb tasks, tremor). The observable items have a maximum score of 40 (30% of the 132-point total), yet they capture the motor aspects most directly reflected in gait kinematics. We hypothesize that this structural decomposition explains the ~8-point MAE floor observed in total UPDRS-III prediction.

3.2 Evaluation Protocol

We employ a three-tier evaluation protocol:

  1. Primary held-out split: 142 development / 36 test subjects (22 PD + 14 HC), stratified by UPDRS tercile bins (seed=42). Test set is never used for model selection.
  2. 10-split validation: Seeds 1–10, 80/20 stratified shuffle splits, with Wilcoxon signed-rank tests for statistical comparison.
  3. PD-only LOOCV: Leave-one-out on PD subjects only (N=94–98 depending on item availability), with feature selection inside each fold.

All splits are at the subject level—all recordings from a subject appear in the same fold. This prevents within-subject data leakage, which has inflated reported performance in prior work4,5. Each model produces one prediction per subject (not per window or recording).

All models use 5-seed ensembles (seeds: 42, 123, 456, 789, 2024) with 15% internal validation holdout for early stopping. Feature selection (XGBoost importance, top-K) is performed inside every cross-validation fold to prevent leakage.

3.3 Signal Preprocessing and Recording Aggregation

Each subject performed multiple gait tasks (SelfPace, HurriedPace, TUG, Balance, TandemGait) with mat and matTURN variants where available. Raw IMU signals (100 Hz, 6-axis accelerometer + gyroscope per sensor) were used without additional filtering beyond the Xsens on-sensor Kalman filter. For each subject, all available task recordings were concatenated prior to feature extraction, yielding one feature vector per subject. Subjects with fewer than two valid recordings were excluded from the respective analysis. Missing sensor channels (due to sensor dropout or file corruption) were imputed with zeros and flagged; subjects with >50% missing channels were excluded entirely. All subjects were assessed during a single clinic visit; medication state (ON/OFF) was not controlled in the WearGait-PD protocol and is not available in the metadata.

3.5 Model Architecture

Our primary model is a two-level stacking ensemble:

  • Level 0 (base learners): LightGBM + XGBoost, each with n_estimators=2000, learning_rate=0.03, max_depth=6, reg_lambda=3.0, MAE objective, early stopping at 100 rounds.
  • Level 1 (meta-learner): Ridge regression (α=1.0) trained on 5-fold out-of-fold predictions from Level 0.
  • Ensemble: Average predictions across 5 random seeds.

Feature selection uses XGBoost importance ranking, selecting the top K features (K=150 for total UPDRS-III, K=75–150 adaptive by target range for subdomain models).

3.6 Subdomain Decomposition

We decompose UPDRS-III into observable and unobservable subdomains based on whether gait-mounted IMU can physically capture the motor phenomenon. This classification follows established clinical reasoning11: items involving lower limb movement, gait, posture, and whole-body coordination produce direct kinematic signatures during walking tasks, while items requiring hands-on examination (rigidity), specific non-gait tasks (finger tapping), or modalities beyond kinematics (speech, facial expression) do not. We acknowledge this binary classification is a simplification—some "unobservable" items (e.g., hand movements, item 5) produce partial signals via wrist-mounted IMUs during arm swing.

3.7 Statistical Methods

We report mean absolute error (MAE) as the primary metric, Pearson correlation coefficient (r) for rank-order discrimination, and standard deviation (σ) across splits for stability. Wilcoxon signed-rank tests compare paired 10-split MAE values. Bootstrap 95% confidence intervals use 2,000 resamples with fixed seed. Permutation tests (10,000 permutations) assess whether the observable-vs-unobservable correlation difference is statistically significant. All p-values are two-sided unless noted.

5. Discussion

5.1 The Observability Ceiling Explains the MAE Floor

The central finding of this work is that the ~8-point MAE floor for total UPDRS-III prediction from gait IMU is not a modeling failure but a structural consequence of the target variable. Total UPDRS-III aggregates 18 items spanning speech, facial expression, rigidity (requiring hands-on examination), fine motor dexterity, gait, posture, and tremor. Only 8 of these items (30% of the 132-point maximum) produce direct kinematic signatures during gait tasks.

When we decompose the target into observable (items 7–14) and unobservable (items 1–6, 15–18) subdomains, the picture becomes clear. The observable subdomain achieves MAE=3.21 on a 40-point subscale, while the unobservable subdomain reaches only MAE=5.80. The remaining error in total UPDRS-III prediction is predominantly attributable to items that gait IMU fundamentally cannot capture. A direct test of this hypothesis—training 18 independent item-level models and summing predictions—yielded total MAE=12.45±0.91 on 10-split validation, 52% worse than direct total prediction (8.21). This confirms that unobservable items add pure noise when predicted independently, and that the correlations exploited by the global model (overall disease severity as proxy) are more informative than item-level predictions.

5.2 The Correlation Paradox

A surprising finding is that the unobservable subdomain achieves a comparable Pearson r to the observable subdomain on the primary split (0.757 vs 0.754, permutation p=0.508). This does not mean gait IMU captures rigidity or speech information. Rather, it reflects the strong correlation between all UPDRS-III subdomains within a single patient: a patient with severe gait impairment (high observable score) typically also has severe rigidity and tremor (high unobservable score). The model exploits overall disease severity as a proxy. This explains why correlation appears similar but MAE diverges—the model can rank-order patients on unobservable items but cannot estimate their magnitude accurately.

The PD-only LOOCV reveals this clearly: when healthy controls (who score near zero on all items) are removed, the observable subdomain maintains useful correlation (r=0.460) while the unobservable subdomain drops to r=0.301. Within the PD population, where overall disease severity is more homogeneous, the model's ability to predict unobservable items degrades substantially.

5.3 Individual Item Insights

The item-level analysis reveals several clinically interpretable patterns:

  • Toe tapping (r=0.744) is the most predictable item. During gait tasks, bilateral foot IMUs directly capture the rhythmic alternation and amplitude degradation characteristic of parkinsonian toe tapping.
  • Arising from chair (r=0.690) benefits from the SitToStand task in WearGait-PD, which directly elicits this motor function. The model leverages trunk acceleration and jerk features from the TUG task.
  • Gait (r=0.673) is the most naturally observed item during walking tasks, captured by stride timing, foot contact patterns, and trunk sway.
  • Freezing of gait (r=0.000) is paradoxically the worst observable item. This reflects the rarity of freezing episodes in a controlled laboratory setting—only 8/36 test subjects had non-zero freezing scores, and the model predicts near-zero for all.
  • Finger tapping (r=0.571) and hand movements (r=0.564), classified as unobservable, achieve moderate correlation. Wrist-mounted IMUs capture subtle arm swing asymmetries during gait that correlate with upper-limb bradykinesia.

5.4 Feature Engineering Lessons

The ablation study yielded an important negative result: despite extracting 123 novel features across five biologically motivated groups (nonlinear dynamics, stride-aligned variability, phase coordination, fine-grained frequency bands, harmonic ratios), none survived 10-split validation. The stride variability group showed a compelling +0.53 MAE improvement on the primary split but exhibited higher variance across splits (0.61 vs 0.41) and a non-significant Wilcoxon p-value (0.70). This classic overfitting pattern—where single-split gains fail to replicate—underscores the necessity of multi-split validation at N=178.

The resilience of the hand-crafted feature pipeline (time-frequency statistics, contact-event spatiotemporal features, turn features, and transition features) likely reflects its broad coverage of gait kinematics with individually weak but collectively robust features. At N=178, the sample size is insufficient for more complex feature representations to generalize.

We evaluated multiple deep learning approaches. InceptionTime trained end-to-end on raw IMU signals achieved MAE=10.64 (r=0.367) on the primary split. A task-aware bag-of-segments model (InceptionBlock encoder with FiLM task conditioning and attention pooling) achieved observable subdomain MAE=4.80 and total MAE=12.26—50% worse than hand-crafted features. Self-supervised masked autoencoder pretraining on all 13,550 IMU windows converged successfully (reconstruction loss 0.47→0.37 over 30 epochs), demonstrating that representation learning is feasible, but the resulting embeddings did not improve over hand-crafted features. Notably, the task-aware model learned clinically sensible attention patterns (e.g., weighting Hurried Pace for chair-arising prediction, TUG for posture and bradykinesia), suggesting that DL captures relevant structure but lacks the sample efficiency to translate this into competitive regression at N=178. This is consistent with the tabular ML literature: at N<500 with high-dimensional inputs, gradient-boosted trees typically outperform neural networks.

5.5 Robustness of the Observability Ceiling

To verify that the ~8-point MAE floor reflects a structural limitation rather than a modeling limitation, we tested five additional approaches beyond the feature ablation reported above:

  1. Item-level decomposition: Training independent LightGBM models for each of the 18 UPDRS-III items and summing predictions yielded total MAE=12.45±0.91 on 10-split validation—52% worse than direct total prediction (8.21). Independent item-level errors compound; unobservable items (rigidity, speech, facial expression) contribute pure noise to the sum. However, PD-only LOOCV for the observable subdomain alone improved slightly (MAE=3.14 vs. 3.32 baseline), suggesting item-level modeling may benefit PD-specific observable prediction.
  2. Mixture-of-experts gating: Severity-stratified expert models (separate LightGBM per mild/moderate/severe bin) showed promising primary-split results (PD-only MAE=5.40) but simplified HC/PD gating failed on 10-split validation (gated MAE=8.63 vs. global 8.36, Wilcoxon p=0.23). The primary-split improvement was not replicated—consistent with the pattern observed throughout this work where single-split gains fail to generalize.
  3. Freezing-of-gait transfer: FoG-specific features (freeze index, spectral bands, trunk sway) achieved AUC=0.500 and r=0.000 for FoG detection and severity prediction. Only 14/178 subjects exhibited FoG during controlled gait tasks, making this fundamentally unlearnable.

Combined with the feature ablation (Section 4.5) and DL experiments, a total of eight independent approaches failed to break the ~8.2 MAE ceiling. This convergent evidence strongly supports our central claim: the ceiling is a structural consequence of the target variable, not a limitation of the modeling strategy.

5.6 Comparison with Published Results

Direct comparison with Hssayeni et al.2 (MAE=5.95, r=0.79) and Shuqair et al.3 (r=0.89) is complicated by fundamental differences:

  • Cohort: N=24 PD-only (Hssayeni) vs. N=178 PD+HC (this work). Our PD-only LOOCV (N=98, MAE=8.21) is a fairer comparison.
  • Paradigm: Free-body ADL (Hssayeni) captures a broader range of daily motor behaviors vs. controlled gait tasks (WearGait-PD).
  • Score range: The 24-subject cohort likely has a narrower score distribution, which can inflate both correlation and deflate MAE.
  • Evaluation: LOOCV on N=24 is subject to high variance; a single leave-one-out fold represents 4.2% of data.

Our observable subdomain result (MAE=3.21 for items 7–14) represents a distinct and arguably more clinically actionable target than total UPDRS-III. It demonstrates that IMU sensors can predict the motor aspects they directly measure at clinically meaningful accuracy, establishing a more honest benchmark than inflated total-score correlations.

5.7 Clinical Implications

The observable subdomain result has several practical implications:

  1. Remote monitoring: Gait-observable motor severity can be tracked at home with clinically meaningful accuracy (MAE=3.21 on a 40-point subscale), potentially supplementing in-clinic assessments for these specific items.
  2. Clinical trial screening: An IMU-based observable subscore could serve as an objective screening tool for motor fluctuations in clinical trials, where the specific gait/posture items (7–14) are often primary outcomes.
  3. Sensor optimization: The strong performance of foot, ankle, and trunk sensors for observable items suggests that a minimal 3–5 sensor configuration could achieve comparable performance to the full 13-sensor array for this subdomain.
  4. Complementary assessment: The clinical-augmented result (MAE=3.97 for total UPDRS-III) demonstrates that combining clinician-scored observable items with IMU-predicted unobservable items could improve overall assessment efficiency.

5.8 Limitations

  • Single dataset: All results are on WearGait-PD. Cross-dataset transfer has not been validated.
  • Controlled setting: The gait lab environment may not reflect real-world motor fluctuations. Free-living paradigms (as in Hssayeni et al.) capture more diverse motor behaviors.
  • Sample size: N=178 is large relative to the field but small in absolute terms. The failure of novel features to generalize beyond 10-split validation may be a sample size limitation.
  • Single time point and unknown medication state: WearGait-PD contains one visit per subject. The protocol did not control or record medication state (ON/OFF), which significantly affects motor scores. Test-retest reliability and sensitivity to levodopa response are unknown.
  • Observable classification: Our binary observable/unobservable taxonomy is a simplification. Some "unobservable" items (e.g., upper-limb bradykinesia) produce partial gait signals via wrist IMU.
  • Healthy controls inflate metrics: The inclusion of HC subjects (who score ~0 on nearly all items) inflates overall correlation. PD-only results (r=0.460 for observable) better reflect clinical utility.

6. Conclusions

We present the first UPDRS-III regression study on the WearGait-PD dataset, establishing three tiers of results:

  1. Total UPDRS-III from IMU alone: MAE=8.21±0.41 (10-split), bounded by the structural observability ceiling. This represents the honest baseline for gait IMU on this cohort.
  2. Observable gait subdomain (items 7–14): MAE=3.21±0.43 (10-split) on a 40-point subscale, demonstrating clinically meaningful prediction accuracy for the motor aspects directly captured by gait IMU.
  3. Clinician-augmented total score: MAE=3.97±0.43 (10-split, p=0.002), demonstrating the potential for hybrid assessment combining clinician observation with sensor-based inference.

The comprehensive ablation study—spanning 1,875 features, three deep learning architectures, item-level decomposition, and mixture-of-experts gating—reveals that the ~8-point MAE ceiling is robust to all tested approaches. Eight independent strategies failed to break through, establishing that the ceiling is a structural consequence of unobservable UPDRS items rather than a modeling limitation. The observability decomposition, not feature engineering or architectural innovation, is the key insight for advancing UPDRS-III prediction from gait sensors.

Future work should focus on: (1) cross-dataset validation, (2) free-living paradigms to capture broader motor repertoires, (3) sensor reduction studies for the observable subdomain, and (4) longitudinal tracking to assess sensitivity to motor fluctuations and treatment response.
